Somali Grammar

Somali is an agglutinative language, using many affixes and particles to determine and alter the meaning of words. As in other related Afroasiatic languages, Somali nouns are inflected for gender, number and case, while verbs are inflected for persons, number, tenses, and moods. Affixes change according to a number of rules. The definite article is a suffix, with the basic form being -ki or -ka for masculine nouns and -ti or -ta for feminine nouns. The k or t is the actual article marker, although it can change depending on the preceding consonant, with the following vowel determined by the case of the noun. Articles do not change for singular or plural.

History of Somali Language

The Somali language has a rich history, including its origins, its status as an official language, and its writing system: The Somali language originated in the Horn of Africa, likely in the first millennium BCE. It's part of the Afroasiatic language family and is classified as an East Cushitic language. Somali became the official language of Somalia in 1972. Before then, English and Italian were used in government, and English was the primary language in schools. Somali was primarily spoken until 1973, when Somalia officially adopted a writing system based on the Latin alphabet. Today, some people also use non-Latin alphabets to write Somali, such as the Arabic alphabet. The exact age of the Somali language is difficult to pinpoint, but it's certainly thousands of years old. Some historians estimate that it originated between 6,000 and 10,000 BC.
